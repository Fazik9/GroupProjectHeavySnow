Heavy Snowfall in a Warming Climate

Warmer oceans can load the atmosphere with more moisture. When cold air intrudes, that moisture can fall as extreme snow. This page highlights why heavy snow events are not a contradiction to global warming, the impacts on people and infrastructure, and how communities can adapt.

6 quick facts

More moisture, bigger dumps

For each degree of ocean warming, the air can hold more water vapor. If surface air is below freezing, heavy snow can result.

Lake‑effect intensification

Warmer, ice‑free lakes feed narrow snow bands downwind that can produce several feet of snow in hours.

Rain‑on‑snow hazards

Mixed storms that start as snow and turn to rain can load roofs, trigger floods, and create severe ice layers.

Power and transport hit first

Heavy, wet snow snaps branches and lines, while low visibility and drifts close roads, ports, and runways.

Avalanche risk can spike

Rapid loading on weak snowpack layers increases the likelihood of natural and human‑triggered slides.

Not every winter is bigger

Long‑term warming shortens the average snow season in many regions, yet extremes can still occur more often.

Adaptation ideas

Stronger roofs and codes

Update design snow loads and prioritize critical facilities like hospitals and shelters.

Tree and line management

Clearance programs and undergrounding reduce outages from heavy, wet snow.

Smarter operations

Targeted snow routes, real‑time signage, and staged closures improve safety during peak bands.
